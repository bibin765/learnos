# Roadmap: World War 2

> Generated on 2026-04-22 via `templates/roadmap-gen.md` (C.R.E.A.T.E. framework).
> Target audience: a working professional with ~8 hrs/week who wants to go from pop-history familiarity to being able to reason causally about WW2 and its long-tail consequences.

## Week 1 — Origins: why a second war was already loaded into the first

**Outcome:** Explain, without notes, the causal chain from the Treaty of Versailles (1919) to the German invasion of Poland (1939) in under 3 minutes.

**Core concepts (3–5):**
- **Versailles reparations & the "War Guilt" clause** → structural humiliation primes revanchist politics. Source: Keynes, *The Economic Consequences of the Peace* (1919), ch. 3 & 6.
- **Weimar hyperinflation and the 1929 Depression** → why democracies fail when the middle class loses savings twice in a decade. Source: Tooze, *The Wages of Destruction*, ch. 1.
- **Treaty of Rapallo & failed collective security** → the League of Nations as a machine without an engine. Source: primary — League Covenant Articles 10–16.
- **Appeasement (Munich 1938)** → not stupidity, but a rational bet made on bad information. Source: Chamberlain's Commons speech, 3 Oct 1938 (Hansard).

**Build:**
- A one-page causal diagram (nodes = events, edges = causal claims with dates). Commit as `artifacts/week1-causal-map.md`.

**Feynman checkpoint:**
- "Why was WW2 *not* simply 'WW1 round two,' and what genuinely new forces were in play?"

**Common traps:**
- Treating Hitler's rise as inevitable — it wasn't; the Nov 1932 elections showed Nazi support *declining*.
- Conflating "appeasement" with "cowardice"; read the 1938 British military assessments first.
- Ignoring the Pacific theatre origins (Japan's 1931 Manchuria invasion) — the war did not start in 1939 for Asia.

## Week 2 — The war itself: theatres, turning points, technology

**Outcome:** Identify the 4–5 hinge moments where the Axis lost the strategic initiative, and explain *why* each was the hinge.

**Core concepts (3–5):**
- **Three theatres, one war** → European, Eastern, Pacific each had different logics. Source: Weinberg, *A World at Arms*, intro + ch. 4.
- **The Eastern Front as the decisive theatre** → ~75% of Wehrmacht casualties occurred here. Source: Glantz & House, *When Titans Clashed*, ch. 2.
- **Logistics over tactics** → the Red Ball Express, U-boat tonnage war, Japanese oil starvation. Source: van Creveld, *Supplying War*, ch. 6.
- **Radar, codebreaking, and the Manhattan Project** → industrial science as a strategic asset. Source: Hinsley, *British Intelligence in the Second World War*, vol. 1, ch. 1 (abridged).
- **Total war doctrine** → when civilians become legitimate targets by policy, not accident. Source: Overy, *The Bombing War*, ch. 1.

**Build:**
- A timeline that pins the 5 hinge events (Battle of Britain, Moscow Dec '41, Midway, Stalingrad, D-Day) with a 3-sentence counterfactual for each. Commit as `artifacts/week2-hinges.md`.

**Feynman checkpoint:**
- "If Germany had never invaded the USSR in June 1941, does the war still end in Allied victory? Defend your answer."

**Common traps:**
- Over-crediting D-Day as the decisive moment (it mattered, but Kursk in July 1943 had already broken the Wehrmacht).
- Treating the atomic bombs in isolation from the Soviet entry into the Pacific war (9 Aug 1945).
- Under-weighting the Sino-Japanese war (1937–1945) — Japan was already bleeding before Pearl Harbor.

## Week 3 — The Holocaust and the ethics of total war

**Outcome:** Describe how a modern industrial state organized genocide, and articulate which wartime Allied decisions remain ethically contested and why.

**Core concepts (3–5):**
- **The path from persecution to extermination** → Wannsee Conference (Jan 1942) as bureaucratic hinge, not origin. Source: primary — Wannsee Protocol (translated).
- **Bystanders, perpetrators, rescuers** → Browning's *Ordinary Men* on Reserve Police Battalion 101.
- **The Allied bombing campaigns** → Dresden, Hamburg, Tokyo: military necessity, strategic theatre, or war crime? Source: Grayling, *Among the Dead Cities*, ch. 4.
- **The atomic decision** → read Truman's diary entries (25 July 1945) alongside the Franck Report (June 1945).

**Build:**
- A 600-word essay: *"Which single Allied wartime decision do I find most ethically contested, and what would I have needed to know at the time to decide differently?"* Commit as `artifacts/week3-ethics-essay.md`.

**Feynman checkpoint:**
- "Explain the Holocaust to a smart 14-year-old without reducing it to 'Nazis were evil.' What are the *mechanisms* — bureaucratic, technological, social — that made industrialized genocide possible?"

**Common traps:**
- Framing the Holocaust as uniquely German rather than as one (extreme) instance of patterns (bureaucratic obedience, dehumanizing language, technological amplification) visible elsewhere.
- Retrofitting knowledge: Allied leaders did *not* have clear intelligence of the death camps before 1944. Check what was actually known, when.
- Treating ethics as a scorecard. The hard work is reconstructing the decision under the information and pressure of the moment.

## Week 4 — Aftermath: the world the war actually made

**Outcome:** Trace at least three features of the contemporary world (institutional, geopolitical, technological) directly back to WW2 design choices made in 1944–1948.

**Core concepts (3–5):**
- **Bretton Woods (1944) & the dollar system** → why the USD is still the reserve currency. Source: Steil, *The Battle of Bretton Woods*, ch. 7–8.
- **The UN, NATO, and the security architecture** → designed *against* the failures of the League. Source: primary — UN Charter, Articles 1, 2, 51.
- **Decolonization as a WW2 side-effect** → European empires were bankrupt; India (1947), Indonesia (1949), Vietnam (1945→1954) flow from this.
- **The Cold War as WW2's unfinished business** → Yalta (Feb 1945) as partition, not peace. Source: Gaddis, *The Cold War: A New History*, ch. 1.
- **The technological long tail** → computing (Bletchley, ENIAC), jet aviation, nuclear power, antibiotics at scale, operations research.

**Build:**
- A written piece (800–1200 words) titled *"One institution I use every week that exists because of WW2, and how it would be different if WW2 had ended differently."* Commit as `artifacts/week4-longtail.md`. Share with one other human for feedback.

**Feynman checkpoint:**
- "Walk me through how a decision made in a room in 1944 shapes something I touched today."

**Common traps:**
- Treating 1945 as a clean ending. The Chinese Civil War, Greek Civil War, Indochina, and Palestine are all direct WW2 continuations.
- Over-attributing modern tech to WW2 (the war *accelerated*, it did not invent, most of it — radar, jets, computing, and nukes all had prewar origins).
- Ignoring the non-Western perspective. The war's end in Asia looked nothing like VE Day in Europe.

## Mastery Signal

1. **You catch a casual analogy and sharpen it.** When a colleague says "this is another Munich," you can respond with the actual Munich calculus and whether the analogy holds — or where it breaks.
2. **You reach for causation, not chronology.** In discussion, you stop narrating "and then…" and start saying "because X had happened, Y became the only remaining option for Z" — and you can cite the source.
3. **You resist moral shortcuts without losing moral clarity.** You can describe *why* otherwise-ordinary people participated in atrocity without excusing it, and you can critique Allied decisions without falling into false-equivalence.
